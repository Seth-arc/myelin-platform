# Artisan Training Platform — Foundation Curriculum
## Desktop-Based Online Training for Underprivileged Communities
### Curriculum Design Document v2.0

---

> **Design Philosophy**
> This curriculum is built on a scaffolded, mastery-based progression model anchored in the Bloom's Taxonomy revised framework and the 4C/ID (Four-Component Instructional Design) model for complex skill acquisition. At its cognitive core runs a single unifying principle: **first principles thinking** — the discipline of breaking every problem down to its fundamental, physically verifiable truths and rebuilding understanding from the ground up, rather than inheriting assumptions or copying what was done before.
>
> First principles thinking is the cognitive engine beneath every other tool in this curriculum. It explains *why* the 5 Whys works, *why* the hierarchy of controls is ordered as it is, *why* a spirit level functions, and *why* a permit-to-work exists. Learners who internalise it do not just follow procedures — they understand what the procedures are protecting against at the level of physical and human reality. This makes them better artisans, safer workers, and more autonomous professionals.
>
> Simulation exercises leverage 3D environments to bridge the gap between conceptual understanding and physical practice — critical for learners with limited prior exposure to industrial or technical contexts.

## Module Overview

This module introduces learners to the foundational cognitive skills that underpin all technical work. Before a learner picks up a tool, they must first learn to think like a technician — to observe systematically, reason through cause and effect, ask structured questions, and apply a repeatable methodology when something goes wrong.

The module opens with a direct introduction to **first principles thinking** as a named, teachable habit of mind. This is not framed as abstract philosophy — it is presented as the specific cognitive move that separates artisans who keep fixing the same fault from those who fix it once and understand why it happened. Every subsequent unit builds on this foundation: the IPO model is an application of first principles to systems; the 5 Whys is first principles applied sequentially in time; structured troubleshooting methodologies are first principles reasoning made efficient; the STAR problem statement is first principles applied to documentation.

The 3D simulation exercises allow learners to practice first principles reasoning in a safe, low-stakes environment before applying it on the job.

## UNIT FND-TT-01.0 — Reasoning from First Principles

**Duration:** 2 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Understand, Apply
**Delivery:** Narrated conceptual instruction, worked examples, reflective response task
**Position in module:** This unit is completed first. It establishes the cognitive lens through which all subsequent units are viewed.

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Define first principles thinking in plain language and contrast it with assumption-based reasoning
- Apply the QTB Framework to a familiar non-technical situation
- Identify at least two assumptions they currently hold about technical work and subject each to a first principles challenge

### Why This Unit Comes First

Most people approach unfamiliar situations by analogy: "This looks like what happened last time, so I'll do what I did last time." For routine work, this is efficient. For novel faults, complex systems, or situations where "last time" was wrong, it is exactly what keeps problems recurring.

First principles thinking is the alternative: strip away everything that was told to you, everything assumed, everything inherited — and ask only what you can directly observe and verify to be physically true. Then, and only then, reason upward to a diagnosis and a solution.

Every framework in this module — the IPO model, the 5 Whys, structured troubleshooting, the STAR problem statement — is an application of this one idea. Learning them without first understanding first principles is like learning the steps of a dance without hearing the music. The steps will work until something unexpected happens. The music is always there.

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 0.1 — What Is First Principles Thinking? (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The difference between reasoning by analogy ("it's like...") and reasoning from first principles ("what is actually true here?")
- Where the idea comes from — Aristotle's concept of "first principles" as the foundational truths from which all other knowledge is derived; its modern application in engineering and problem-solving
- Why it is not just for scientists or engineers — it is a daily cognitive tool for any person who works with systems that can fail
- The central risk of assumption-based reasoning: you inherit not only someone's knowledge but also their errors

**The Core Distinction:**

```
REASONING BY ANALOGY:
  "Last time the lights went off it was the main breaker.
   This must be the main breaker again."
  
  Risk: The assumption was correct last time. It may not be
        correct this time. You will replace the breaker and
        the lights will still be off. You wasted 40 minutes
        and one component.

REASONING FROM FIRST PRINCIPLES:
  "The lights are off. What do I know for certain?
   I know lights require electrical energy. I know energy
   flows from a source, through a path, to a load.
   I know the source (mains supply) is working because the
   neighbour's building has power. Therefore the fault is
   somewhere in the path between the source and these lights.
   I know nothing else for certain yet. Let me find out."

  Advantage: Every step is grounded in what is verifiably
             true. No energy is wasted on incorrect assumptions.
```

**Plain Language Explanation for Learners:**

> *"First principles thinking means this: before you do anything, ask yourself — what do I actually know is true here, not what do I think or what was I told? Start only from what you can see, measure, or know for certain. Then build your understanding from there. It is the difference between fixing a problem and fixing the symptom of a problem."*

**Instructional Approach:**
- Open with a 4-minute narrated story: a young mechanic who replaces a car battery three times in one year because that is what his uncle always did. The real problem was a parasitic drain from a faulty interior light relay. Walk through what a first principles approach would have revealed after the first failure.
- Present the Core Distinction above with narrated annotation
- Discussion prompt: *"Think of something you do or believe because 'that's how it's done' or 'that's what I was taught.' Have you ever asked why? What would you need to verify to know if it's actually true?"*

#### Lesson 0.2 — The QTB Framework (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- A three-step, learner-facing structure for applying first principles thinking to any technical situation
- How QTB maps onto the broader problem-solving and troubleshooting tools introduced in later units
- Practice applying QTB to two non-technical scenarios before applying it to a technical one

**The QTB Framework:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     THE QTB FRAMEWORK                           │
│            First Principles Thinking for Artisans               │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│  Q — QUESTION THE ASSUMPTION                                     │
│                                                                  │
│  Ask: "What am I taking for granted here?"                       │
│       "What did someone tell me that I haven't verified?"        │
│       "What am I assuming is normal that I should confirm?"      │
│                                                                  │
│  The goal: Surface every assumption before acting on it.         │
│  Unexamined assumptions are where most diagnostic errors hide.   │
│                                                                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│  T — FIND THE PHYSICAL TRUTH                                     │
│                                                                  │
│  Ask: "What can I directly observe, measure, or verify           │
│        to be true right now?"                                    │
│       "What does the physical evidence actually tell me —        │
│        not what do I think it tells me?"                         │
│                                                                  │
│  The goal: Build a list of confirmed facts. Only facts.          │
│  Everything else is hypothesis until tested.                     │
│                                                                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                  │
│  B — BUILD UP FROM THERE                                         │
│                                                                  │
│  Ask: "Starting only from what I know is true,                   │
│        what does that tell me about this problem?"               │
│       "What is the minimum set of conditions that would          │
│        produce exactly this set of symptoms?"                    │
│                                                                  │
│  The goal: Reconstruct your understanding of the problem         │
│  from the ground up — not downward from a guess.                 │
│                                                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**QTB Applied to a Non-Technical Scenario (Worked Example):**

```
SITUATION: You prepared a meal yesterday using the same recipe you
           always use. Today it tastes wrong.

Q — QUESTION THE ASSUMPTION:
    "I assumed all the ingredients were the same as usual."
    "I assumed my stove temperature hasn't changed."
    "I assumed the recipe is always reliable."

T — FIND THE PHYSICAL TRUTH:
    "I taste something sour that wasn't there before."
    "I open the tin of tomatoes — the tin was dented and
     had a slightly off smell. That is a confirmed fact."
    "Everything else I cooked was fine."

B — BUILD UP FROM THERE:
    "A dented, compromised tin caused spoilage.
     The recipe and stove are fine. The fault is one
     specific ingredient. Solution: replace the tin.
     Prevention: check tins before purchasing."
```

**QTB Applied to a Technical Scenario (Worked Example):**

```
SITUATION: A solar-powered clinic light is not working.

Q — QUESTION THE ASSUMPTION:
    "I assumed the bulb is blown — that is always the
     first thing people check. But is it?"
    "I assumed the panel is working because it was
     installed last month. Can I verify that?"
    "I assumed there was enough sun today. Was there?"

T — FIND THE PHYSICAL TRUTH:
    "I measure voltage at the light fitting: 0V. Confirmed."
    "I measure voltage at the battery terminals: 12.4V. Confirmed.
     The battery has charge. That is a physical fact."
    "I trace the circuit: battery → fuse → switch → light.
     I test continuity at the fuse: open circuit. Confirmed.
     The fuse is blown. That is a physical fact."
    "Everything between the fuse and the battery is fine.
     The light and wiring beyond the fuse are untested."

B — BUILD UP FROM THERE:
    "The fuse is blown. Before I replace it, I must ask:
     what caused it to blow? Simply replacing it without
     knowing why would be fixing the symptom, not the cause.
     I need to test the load current before restoring power."
```

**Instructional Approach:**
- Present the QTB framework visually as the box above, narrated
- Walk through the non-technical worked example first — this lowers the barrier to entry for learners with no technical background
- Walk through the technical worked example with pause points: at each step, ask "Why do we stop here? What assumption did we catch? What physical fact do we have?"
- Emphasise: QTB is not a rigid checklist. It is a habit of mind. With practice it becomes the automatic first response to any unfamiliar situation.

**Key Vocabulary:**
- **Assumption:** Something accepted as true without direct verification
- **Physical truth:** An observable, measurable fact about the current state of a system
- **First principles:** The most fundamental truths of a system — what must be true for it to exist and function at all

## UNIT FND-TT-01.1 — What Is a Technical Problem?

**Duration:** 4 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Remember, Understand
**Delivery:** Video narration, interactive slides, knowledge check

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Define the term "technical problem" in plain language
- Distinguish between a problem, a fault, a defect, and a failure
- Explain why clear problem identification is the first step in any repair or installation task
- Apply the Q step of QTB to challenge assumptions about what constitutes "normal" operation

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.1.1 — Defining Technical Problems (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- What makes something a "technical problem" vs. a general problem
- The cost of ignoring technical problems in real-world settings (safety, money, time)
- Examples drawn from familiar environments: a household tap that drips, a torch that flickers, a plug that sparks

**Instructional Approach:**
- Begin with a 3-minute narrated scenario: a township workshop where a fridge compressor fails the night before a community event. Ask learners: *What went wrong? When did the problem actually start?*
- Present the four vocabulary terms as a visual comparison chart
- Use a sorting drag-and-drop activity where learners categorise 10 real-world examples as problem / fault / defect / failure

**Key Vocabulary:**
- **Problem:** A situation where something is not working as expected and action is required
- **Fault:** A specific malfunction or deviation from normal operation in a component or system
- **Defect:** A flaw introduced during manufacturing or installation that may not yet have caused failure
- **Failure:** The point at which a component or system can no longer perform its intended function

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Unit 1.1**
>
> Before you can identify a problem, you must know what "normal" looks like. Most people assume they know what normal is. Apply the Q step here:
>
> *"Am I certain I know what this system is supposed to do? Where did that knowledge come from — direct verification, the manual, or 'someone told me'?"*
>
> A fridge that runs continuously might be "normal" for a failing compressor — the owner has stopped noticing. A technician applying first principles asks: what is the rated duty cycle of this compressor? What should the temperature in this fridge be? Only then can "this fridge is running wrong" be stated as a confirmed physical fact rather than a hunch.
>
> **First principles rule for problem definition:** "Normal" is not what you are used to seeing. Normal is what the system specification says it should be doing under the current conditions. Always verify your baseline.

#### Lesson 1.1.2 — Why the First Step Is the Hardest (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Human tendency to jump to solutions before understanding the problem
- The cost of misidentification (wasted parts, wasted labour, making things worse)
- The difference between what we see (symptom) and what caused it (introduced here as a preview)

**Instructional Approach:**
- Show two short animated vignettes: one where a learner jumps straight to replacing a fuse (wrong), one where a learner pauses to ask questions first (right)
- Facilitate a reflective prompt: *Think of a time you or someone you know fixed something but it broke again. What might have been missed?*
- Present the "Problem Identification Checklist" — a simple 5-item tool learners will use throughout the module

**Problem Identification Checklist (introduced):**
1. What exactly is not working?
2. When did it stop working?
3. Has anything changed recently?
4. Does it happen all the time or only sometimes?
5. Is there anything else behaving strangely at the same time?

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Problem Identification Checklist**
>
> Each of the five questions on this checklist is a QTB move in disguise:
>
> Question 1 is T — find the physical truth of what is observed.
> Question 2 is Q — challenge the assumption that the problem started now. It may have been developing for weeks.
> Question 3 is Q — what has changed is often where the cause lives.
> Questions 4 and 5 are T — pattern and correlated symptoms are physical evidence. They narrow the fault space before you touch anything.
>
> The checklist forces you to collect confirmed facts before forming a hypothesis. That is first principles in practice.

#### Assessment: Knowledge Check Quiz (30 min)

- 15 multiple-choice questions
- Covers definitions and problem vs. symptom distinction
- Minimum pass: 70%
- Immediate feedback with explanation for incorrect answers

## UNIT FND-TT-01.2 — How Systems Work: Inputs, Processes, Outputs

**Duration:** 5 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Understand, Apply
**Delivery:** Interactive diagram exploration, 3D simulation

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Define the Input-Process-Output (IPO) model and apply it to a technical system
- Identify at least three components within a simple system and describe their function
- Trace how a fault in one component can affect the whole system
- Explain, using first principles, why all energy-handling systems follow the IPO pattern

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.2.1 — The IPO Model (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Systems thinking: the idea that components do not work in isolation
- Input, Process, Output defined with everyday examples
- Feedback loops introduced in simple terms (what the system "tells itself")

**Instructional Approach:**
- Learners interact with an annotated diagram of a simple water pump system
- Clicking on each component reveals its role: what it receives (input), what it does (process), what it sends on (output)
- Learners label three new diagrams independently: a basic electrical circuit, a mechanical pulley, a plumbing line
- Debrief: discussion prompts around what happens when a middle component fails

**Worked Example — Simple Electrical Torch:**

```
Input:           Process:              Output:
Battery          Switch routes         Light emitted
(electrical      current through       from bulb
 energy)         the circuit
```

If the switch is faulty → process is disrupted → output fails even though input (battery) is fine.

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why All Systems Are IPO**
>
> This is not a convention invented by engineers to make diagrams look neat. It is a physical necessity derived from two fundamental laws:
>
> 1. **Conservation of energy:** Energy cannot be created from nothing. Every system must receive energy from somewhere (Input) to produce a useful effect (Output). The Process is whatever transformation happens in between.
>
> 2. **Cause and effect:** Every observable output has a traceable cause. There are no effects without causes. The IPO model is simply a structured way to follow that causal chain.
>
> Apply QTB here: when a system produces no output, the first principles question is not "which part broke?" It is "where in the causal chain from Input to Output did the energy transfer stop?" That single question tells you exactly where to start looking — and you derived it from physical law, not from habit.
>
> **Practical implication:** If you know what the output should be and you can measure whether it is present, you can always narrow a fault to one of three regions: before the process, within the process, or after the process. This is true for every system you will ever work on.

#### Lesson 1.2.2 — Mapping a System (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- How to draw a simple system map (not a technical diagram — a thinking tool)
- Identifying boundaries: where does the system start and end?
- Identifying dependencies: which parts depend on which?

**Instructional Approach:**
- Introduce the "System Map" as a hand-sketch thinking tool, not an engineering diagram
- Learners complete a partially filled system map for a solar panel charge setup (simplified: panel → charge controller → battery → load)
- Identify what would happen if each part were removed or failed
- Pair reflection: learners explain their map to a partner or write a short explanation (asynchronous voice note option for lower-literacy learners)

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — What a System Map Is Really Doing**
>
> When you draw a system map, you are performing the T step of QTB: you are recording only what you can confirm is physically there and physically connected. A system map drawn from memory or assumption is worse than no map — it gives false confidence.
>
> The first principles discipline for system mapping: draw only what you can currently see or have physically traced. Mark components you are assuming are present as "unverified." Before troubleshooting begins, the goal is to convert every "unverified" to a confirmed fact.
>
> This is especially important for solar PV systems, which are often partially concealed in conduit, combiner boxes, and roof mountings. The most experienced solar technicians draw a fresh system map at every new site rather than assuming the installation matches the documentation.

#### 3D Simulation Exercise 01 — System Fault Identification (2 hours)

**Simulation Title:** The Workshop Power Failure
**Environment:** 3D rendered small township electrical workshop, daytime interior, overhead fluorescent lighting, workbench with power tools

**Scenario Brief:**
The lights and power tools in a small workshop have stopped working. The learner enters the simulation and must identify where in the electrical supply chain the fault lies — without touching anything yet.

**Learning Tasks Within the Simulation:**
1. Observe the environment — identify which devices are off and which are on (partial failure pattern)
2. Trace the visible supply path: main DB board → sub-board → sockets → devices
3. Use the in-simulation "System Map Clipboard" tool to annotate what is working and what is not
4. **First Principles Step:** Before touching anything, use the QTB prompts on the in-simulation clipboard to record: (a) at least two assumptions you are making about this system, (b) at least three physical facts you have confirmed by observation alone, and (c) what those facts tell you about where the fault must be
5. Generate a preliminary fault location hypothesis: before the sub-board, at the sub-board, or after it?

**3D Environment Specifications:**
- PBR materials: bare concrete floor, painted brick walls with surface chips, aged fluorescent fittings with flicker animation on one unit
- Interactive objects: DB board panel (hinged door, clickable breakers), power sockets with status indicators, workbench with power tool plugged in
- No penalty for incorrect interaction — learner receives guided prompts after two incorrect attempts
- Ambient audio: distant traffic, fluorescent hum, workshop ambient

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Correct identification of fault location: 35%
- Completeness of system map annotation: 25%
- QTB clipboard entries (assumptions identified, physical facts confirmed): 25%
- Quality of written hypothesis statement: 15%

## UNIT FND-TT-01.3 — Symptoms vs. Root Causes

**Duration:** 5 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Analyse
**Delivery:** Case study library, cause-effect mapping activity

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Distinguish clearly between a symptom and a root cause
- Apply the "5 Whys" technique to a given fault scenario
- Construct a basic cause-and-effect chain for a technical fault
- Explain the structural relationship between the 5 Whys and first principles reasoning

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.3.1 — Symptoms Are Not Problems (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The difference between observable symptoms and underlying causes
- How treating symptoms only leads to recurring faults
- Medical analogy used as a bridge concept: a headache is a symptom; dehydration may be the cause

**Instructional Approach:**
- Present three short case studies, each showing a technician who fixed the symptom but not the cause:
  - Case 1: Replaced a blown fuse three times without asking why it kept blowing
  - Case 2: Topped up oil in an engine without investigating the oil leak
  - Case 3: Replaced a battery in a device that kept draining it due to a background fault
- Learners identify in each case: what was the symptom? What was the likely root cause?

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Symptoms as Incomplete Physical Evidence**
>
> A symptom is not the problem. It is a physical signal that a problem exists somewhere in the system. Treating a symptom without identifying its cause is like painting over a crack in a wall without checking whether the foundation has shifted. The paint covers the evidence; the foundation continues to fail.
>
> From a first principles perspective, every symptom points to a physical state that should not exist. The fault investigation is a process of asking: *what physical conditions would produce exactly this symptom, and no other?* That question eliminates all explanations that do not fit the observed facts — which is the B step of QTB applied to diagnosis.
>
> The experienced artisan's rule: "If you cannot state the physical mechanism by which the root cause produces the symptom you observed, you have not found the root cause yet."

#### Lesson 1.3.2 — The 5 Whys Technique (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Origin and purpose of the 5 Whys (Toyota Production System background, simplified)
- How to apply 5 Whys without going in circles
- Knowing when you have reached the root cause vs. when to keep asking

**The 5 Whys Framework:**

```
SYMPTOM:  The workshop light is not working.

Why 1:    Why is the light not working?
          → The bulb is blown.

Why 2:    Why did the bulb blow?
          → There was a voltage spike.

Why 3:    Why was there a voltage spike?
          → The surge protector was bypassed.

Why 4:    Why was the surge protector bypassed?
          → It tripped repeatedly and the user removed it.

Why 5:    Why did it trip repeatedly?
          → The circuit was overloaded — too many devices on one socket.

ROOT CAUSE: Circuit overloading due to incorrect load calculation at installation.
```

**Instructional Approach:**
- Walk through the above worked example with narration
- Provide a partially completed 5 Whys template for three new scenarios
- Learners complete the chain independently
- Common mistake to highlight: stopping at the first "fixable" cause rather than the true root

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The 5 Whys Is First Principles in Time**
>
> Every "Why?" is a Q step: it challenges the assumption that the current level of explanation is the deepest available. Every answer is a T step: it identifies a more fundamental physical truth. The chain is complete when the answer is a physical or systemic condition that cannot itself be explained by another fault — only by a decision, a design, or a material property.
>
> The test for having reached a true root cause: *"If this condition had not existed, would this fault have occurred?"* If the answer is "No," you have found the cause. If the answer is "It still might have," keep asking.
>
> Notice also that the 5 Whys in the example above ends not with a component but with a decision: incorrect load calculation at installation. Root causes in technical systems very often live at the level of decisions — design choices, installation shortcuts, maintenance omissions. This is where first principles thinking is most powerful: it breaks through the assumption that the problem is always a hardware fault.

#### Lesson 1.3.3 — Cause-and-Effect Mapping (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Introduction to the Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram in simplified form
- Using a cause-and-effect map as a thinking tool, not a documentation requirement
- Four cause categories appropriate for trade context: Equipment, Method, Material, Environment

**Instructional Approach:**
- Present a pre-drawn fishbone for a simple fault (MC4 connector failure in a solar system)
- Learners add at least two possible causes to each branch of a blank fishbone for a new scenario
- Debrief: which causes are most likely? How would you find out?

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The Fishbone as a First Principles Inventory**
>
> The fishbone diagram is a structured way to perform the Q step across multiple domains simultaneously: it asks "what assumptions are we making about Equipment, Method, Material, and Environment?" before committing to a single hypothesis.
>
> The discipline here is: every item on a fishbone branch is a hypothesis, not a fact. The first principles test is to identify which hypotheses can be eliminated by physical evidence that already exists, and which require further testing. After the fishbone is drawn, prioritise the branches not by which cause seems most likely, but by which causes can be tested most quickly and conclusively. This is the B step: build your investigation plan from confirmed facts and testable hypotheses, not from intuition.

**Scenario:** A small solar-powered water pump at a rural clinic has stopped running. The pump was working yesterday. The battery gauge shows 80% charge. The fuse is intact.

2. List five observable symptoms (what you can see, hear, measure)
3. Apply the 5 Whys starting from the most likely symptom
4. Produce a cause-and-effect map with at least 6 possible causes across 3 branches
5. Write a two-paragraph fault hypothesis: what you think the root cause is and why — stated as a physically grounded argument, not an intuition

**Rubric Highlights:**

- Symptoms correctly identified and distinguished from causes: 20%

**Duration:** 7 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Apply, Analyse
**Delivery:** Conceptual instruction, methodology comparison, 3D simulation

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Explain the first principles basis for why each methodology is efficient for specific system types

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.4.1 — Why Methodology Matters (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The difference between random trial-and-error and structured troubleshooting
- How professional technicians and artisans approach unknown faults
- Overview of four methodologies: Half-Split, Top-Down, Bottom-Up, Compare-and-Swap

**Instructional Approach:**
- Show a short animated comparison: a learner using trial-and-error (10 steps, 3 errors, 45 minutes) vs. a learner using half-split (4 steps, no errors, 12 minutes) for the same fault
- Discuss: why does structure matter in a paid job context? (Time = money; safety = non-negotiable)

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why Structure Is More Efficient Than Intuition**
>
> Trial-and-error is not random from the artisan's perspective — each guess feels like intuition. But intuition is compressed assumption. It skips the T step: it does not ask "what physical facts do I have, and what do they tell me?" It asks "what worked last time?" or "what seems likely?"
>
> Structured methodologies are efficient because they are built on a first principles insight: *information is the most valuable resource in a fault investigation, and every test should maximise the information gained per unit of time.* Random guessing produces one data point per test. Half-Split produces a data point that eliminates half the remaining fault space per test. That is not a procedural preference — it is information geometry. Understanding this is what allows an experienced technician to adapt or invent a methodology when the situation is genuinely novel, rather than being helpless because the textbook scenario does not match the real one.

#### Lesson 1.4.2 — The Four Core Methodologies (2 hours)

**Methodology 1: Half-Split (Binary Divide)**

```
Principle: Divide the system into two halves. Test the midpoint.
If the fault is before the midpoint → test the first quarter next.
If the fault is after the midpoint → test the third quarter next.
Continue halving until the fault is isolated.

Best used when: Systems are linear and testable at multiple points
                (e.g., a cable run, a data signal path, a pipe system)

Example: A 20-metre cable run with 10 connectors has a break somewhere.
         Test at connector 5 first. If signal is present, break is in
         the second half. Test at connector 8 next. And so on.
```

> **First Principles Basis — Half-Split:**
> Each test eliminates 50% of the remaining unknowns, regardless of which half the fault is in. A 10-segment system requires a maximum of 4 tests (log₂ of 10 ≈ 3.3, round up). This is the most information-efficient strategy when all positions in the system are equally unknown and the system is testable at its midpoint. If one region of the system is more likely to be faulty based on prior evidence, this adjusts the optimal split point — but the principle remains the same.

**Methodology 2: Top-Down**

```
Principle: Start at the power/signal source (the top of the system)
and work downward toward the load/output.

Best used when: The source is easily accessible and the system is
                hierarchical (e.g., electrical distribution systems,
                plumbing from header tank down)

Risk: Can waste time if the fault is near the output end.
```

> **First Principles Basis — Top-Down:**
> The physical truth of any supply system is that energy flows in one direction: from source to load. If the source is confirmed healthy, the fault must be downstream. Each test confirms a longer section of healthy supply, narrowing the fault zone. This is efficient only when the source is easy to test and likely to be confirmed healthy — which is often the case in distribution systems fed from an established mains supply.

**Methodology 3: Bottom-Up**

```
Principle: Start at the load/output end and work backward toward
the source.

Best used when: The output device is the most recently changed
component, or when the system is fed from multiple sources.

Risk: Can waste time if the fault is near the source.
```

> **First Principles Basis — Bottom-Up:**
> Recent change is a physical fact that narrows the probability distribution of fault location. If a component was just installed or replaced and the system immediately failed, the first principles question is: what physical state change was introduced by that installation? Starting at the output — where the most recent change occurred — is reasoning from the highest-probability physical evidence first.

**Methodology 4: Compare-and-Swap**

```
Principle: Replace a suspected component with a known-good equivalent.
If the fault disappears, the swapped component was the cause.

Best used when: Components are modular and easily interchangeable,
                and a spare is available.

Risk: Does not find the root cause if the swapped component is damaged
      by an underlying fault that remains. Always ask WHY the component
      failed before assuming swap is the permanent fix.
```

> **First Principles Basis — Compare-and-Swap:**
> This method is an empirical falsification test. The hypothesis is: "This component is the cause." The swap creates a controlled change to one variable while holding all others constant. If the output changes, the hypothesis is confirmed. This is the scientific method applied to maintenance. The critical first principles warning: confirming that a component caused the failure does not explain why it failed. A component destroyed by an overvoltage surge will simply be destroyed again if the surge condition is not addressed. Swap confirms the what; first principles reasoning is still required to find the why.

**Instructional Approach:**
- Each methodology is presented as an interactive flowchart — learners click through each decision node
- Worked example for each methodology using the same scenario (a lighting circuit fault), so learners can compare outcomes
- Methodology selection decision matrix introduced:

| Scenario Type | Recommended Methodology | First Principles Rationale |
|---------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Long linear system, multiple test points | Half-Split | Maximises information per test; all positions equally unknown |
| Hierarchical supply system, source easily accessed | Top-Down | Source health is cheapest fact to confirm; narrows downstream |
| Recently installed component at output end | Bottom-Up | Recent change is highest-probability physical evidence |
| Modular system, spares available | Compare-and-Swap | Empirical falsification of single-variable hypothesis |

#### Lesson 1.4.3 — Troubleshooting Documentation (30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Documentation as Physical Truth Record**

```
DATE:         ___________________________

| STEP | WHAT I TESTED         | WHAT I FOUND          | NEXT ACTION              |
```

## UNIT FND-TT-01.5 — Structured Problem-Solving in Practice

**Duration:** 5 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Apply, Create
**Delivery:** Problem-solving framework instruction, independent task

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Apply a complete problem-solving cycle from observation through to proposed solution
- Write a structured problem statement using a standardised format
- Evaluate a proposed solution before implementation using a defined checklist
- Demonstrate how the STAR format and SAFE checklist are instruments of first principles reasoning

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.5.1 — The Problem-Solving Cycle (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The 7-step problem-solving cycle used in technical trades
- The difference between a problem statement and a task instruction
- Why solutions must be evaluated before implementation (safety, cost, reversibility)

**The 7-Step Technical Problem-Solving Cycle:**

```
STEP 1:  OBSERVE     → Gather all available information without touching anything
STEP 2:  DEFINE      → Write a clear problem statement
STEP 3:  ANALYSE     → Identify possible causes (5 Whys, fishbone)
STEP 4:  PLAN        → Select methodology and sequence of tests
STEP 5:  TEST        → Execute the plan, document every step
STEP 6:  EVALUATE    → Assess the proposed solution before acting
STEP 7:  IMPLEMENT   → Carry out the fix; verify the outcome
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The 7-Step Cycle as QTB Made Explicit**
>
> The 7-step cycle is a formalisation of QTB across the full problem-solving arc:
>
> - **Steps 1–2 (Observe, Define)** = T + Q: collect physical facts; surface and challenge assumptions about the system state
> - **Step 3 (Analyse)** = Q continued: challenge every potential cause; test nothing until you have a hypothesis worth testing
> - **Steps 4–5 (Plan, Test)** = B: build your investigation from confirmed facts; each test is a controlled addition to your knowledge
> - **Steps 6–7 (Evaluate, Implement)** = B continued + Q applied to solutions: does this solution address the physical root cause, or only the symptom? Is there any assumption in my proposed fix that I have not verified?
>
> A learner who understands this mapping can construct a problem-solving process for situations they have never encountered before, because the logic is derived from first principles — not memorised from a template.

**The STAR Problem Statement Format (adapted for trade context):**

```
SITUATION:  Describe what the system/device/installation should be doing.
            [This is T — the verified baseline: what is physically supposed to happen?]

TASK:       Describe what it is actually doing (observable symptoms).
            [This is T — what is physically happening right now, confirmed by observation?]

ANOMALY:    Describe the specific difference between expected and actual.
            [This is T — the confirmed deviation: stated as a measurable gap, not a guess]

RISK:       Describe what could go wrong if this is not addressed.
            [This is B — what does the physical evidence tell us about consequences?]
```

**Worked Example:**

```
SITUATION:  The rooftop solar installation on the clinic should supply
            240V AC to the building during daylight hours.

TASK:       The inverter is producing 160V AC and cycling off every
            12 minutes.

ANOMALY:    Voltage output is 33% below rated output; intermittent
            shutdown not consistent with normal load behaviour.

RISK:       Medical refrigeration equipment may be compromised;
            vaccine storage at risk if fault is not resolved within
            4 hours.
```

#### Lesson 1.5.2 — Solution Evaluation Before Action (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Why not every solution should be implemented immediately
- The SAFE checklist for solution evaluation
- Understanding when to escalate vs. when to proceed

**The SAFE Solution Evaluation Checklist:**

```
S — SAFETY:        Does this solution introduce any new hazards?
                   Is the work within my current skill/authority level?

A — ACCURACY:      Does this solution address the root cause (not just the symptom)?
                   Have I tested my hypothesis, not just assumed it?

F — FEASIBILITY:   Do I have the tools, materials, and time to implement this?
                   Do I need to isolate or lock out any systems first?

E — EFFECTS:       What are the downstream effects of this solution?
                   Will other systems or users be affected during the fix?
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The SAFE Checklist as a Q Step for Solutions**
>
> A proposed solution is just another hypothesis. The SAFE checklist applies the Q step to that hypothesis: it forces you to surface and challenge the assumptions embedded in your proposed fix before you act on it.
>
> The A criterion is the most important from a first principles perspective: "Does this solution address the root cause?" If you have reached the root cause through a proper QTB and 5 Whys process, this should be answerable with a "yes" backed by physical evidence. If you cannot answer it confidently, your diagnosis is incomplete.
>
> The E criterion is also a first principles test: it asks you to reason forward from the proposed change through the system to identify downstream physical consequences. This is B applied to the future state of the system. In complex installations, unintended downstream effects — a voltage change affecting a control circuit, a pressure change affecting a downstream valve — are as common a source of new faults as the original problem.

#### Assessment: Written Problem Statement Task (2 hours 30 min)

**Task Brief:**
Learners are presented with a detailed written scenario (1 page) and a set of simulated "observation notes" (photos of equipment in the 3D environment, meter readings, a brief from the site supervisor).

**Deliverables:**
1. QTB pre-work: a bulleted list distinguishing confirmed physical facts (T) from assumptions or unverified claims (Q) in the scenario brief
2. Complete STAR Problem Statement (each field annotated with which QTB step it represents)
3. 5 Whys analysis (minimum 4 whys deep; each Why must identify a new physical mechanism, not restate the previous answer)
4. Selected troubleshooting methodology with written justification citing its first principles rationale
5. Completed SAFE checklist for their proposed solution
6. Brief (max 200 words) explanation of what they would do next and who they would inform

**Assessment Rubric:**
- QTB pre-work clearly distinguishes facts from assumptions: 20%
- STAR statement is complete, accurate, and physically grounded: 25%
- 5 Whys reaches root cause level with physical mechanism stated at each step: 25%
- Methodology selection justified on first principles grounds: 10%
- SAFE checklist demonstrates safety and downstream awareness: 10%
- Escalation decision is correct and appropriately described: 10%

#### Lesson 1.6.1 — Audience-Aware Technical Communication (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Three communication audiences and what each needs: peer (technical detail), supervisor (status + risk + recommendation), client (what it means for them + what happens next)
- Avoiding jargon with non-technical audiences

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Stripping Communication to Fundamentals**
>
> Apply QTB to every communication act before writing or speaking:
>
> Q — What am I assuming this person already knows? What context am I taking for granted? What jargon am I using that they may not understand?
>
> T — What are the physical facts that are genuinely relevant to this person's decision or action? Strip everything else. A supervisor does not need your troubleshooting log — they need risk level, status, and recommendation. A client does not need to know what a bypass diode is — they need to know whether their system is safe and when it will be fixed.
>
> B — Build the message from only what is true and only what is relevant to this specific audience's needs. Anything else is noise that obscures the message.
>
> **The first principles test for any technical communication:** *"If I remove everything from this message that my audience cannot act on or does not need to understand, does the core message survive intact?"* If yes, send it. If not, revise until it does.

**Communication Register Comparison:**

```
SAME FAULT — THREE MESSAGES:

"Panel 3 is showing zero Voc at the terminal. MC4 connectors look
intact but I'm getting no continuity on the positive lead from
junction box to combiner. Likely a break in the cable run or a
failed bypass diode."

TO A SUPERVISOR:
"One of the four solar panels is not producing power. I've identified

```

#### Lesson 1.6.2 — Writing a Fault Report (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The Fault Report as a professional and legal document
- Standard fault report structure
- Common mistakes: vague language, missing measurements, no recommended action

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The Fault Report as a Physical Truth Record**
>
> A fault report is not a narrative account of what you did. It is a structured record of confirmed physical facts (T), the reasoning built from those facts (B), and the actions taken or recommended. Its purpose is to allow any other competent technician — or, in a legal context, any reviewer — to reconstruct your diagnostic reasoning entirely from the written record.
>
> The first principles test for a fault report entry: *"Is this a confirmed fact, or is it an interpretation? If it is an interpretation, have I labelled it as such and stated the evidence on which it is based?"* Vague language in fault reports ("seems to be," "might have been") fails this test — it mixes confirmed facts with ungrounded assumptions and makes the record worthless as a technical document.

**Standard Fault Report Template:**

```
FAULT REPORT

Site/Installation:   ___________________________
Date:                ___________________________
Reported by:         ___________________________
Supervisor:          ___________________________

1. FAULT DESCRIPTION (what was observed — confirmed physical facts only)
   _______________________________________________

2. SYMPTOMS RECORDED (with measurements where applicable)
   _______________________________________________

3. TESTS PERFORMED (list each test and its result)
   _______________________________________________

4. ROOT CAUSE IDENTIFIED (physically grounded statement)
   _______________________________________________

5. CORRECTIVE ACTION TAKEN / RECOMMENDED
   _______________________________________________

6. PARTS USED / REQUIRED
   _______________________________________________

7. REFERRAL REQUIRED? (Yes / No — if yes, to whom)
   _______________________________________________

8. FOLLOW-UP REQUIRED? (Yes / No — describe)
   _______________________________________________

Signature: ___________________  Date: ____________
```

- Apply the Q step of QTB to surface and challenge assumptions brought to a new site

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.1.1 — The Anatomy of a Large Facility (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Types of facilities learners may work in: manufacturing plants, hospitals, mines, data centres, commercial buildings, solar farms, water treatment works
- Major functional zones common to most facilities: reception/gatehouse, administration, production/operations, maintenance workshop, stores/warehouse, utilities (electrical substation, generator room, HVAC plant), hazardous materials storage, waste handling

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why Zones Exist**
>
> Industrial facilities are zoned because different physical and chemical processes produce different hazard profiles, and mixing hazard profiles multiplies risk nonlinearly. A zone is not an administrative invention — it is a physical boundary drawn around a specific set of energy states, substances, or processes that require their own control regime.
>
> Apply Q here: *"What am I assuming about this area based on what it looks like?"* A plant room that appears clean and quiet may contain pressurised systems with stored mechanical energy equivalent to an explosion. A loading dock that appears inactive may have a forklift that starts automatically on a schedule. The physical truth of an industrial environment is often not visible on the surface. This is why zone knowledge is safety-critical.
>
> The first principles question for any new zone: *"What are the forms of energy present here — electrical, thermal, kinetic, potential, chemical, pressure? What physical boundaries contain those energy forms? What happens if those boundaries are breached?"*

#### Lesson 1.1.2 — People on Site: Roles and Responsibilities (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The site hierarchy: site owner/manager, operations manager, safety officer, supervisors, artisans, apprentices, contractors, visitors

- What a contractor is and why contractors face additional scrutiny on industrial sites

**Key Role Definitions:**

```
SITE MANAGER:     Overall authority. Accountable for everything on site.

SAFETY OFFICER:   Enforces safety regulations. Has authority to stop work.
                  Your first point of contact for safety concerns.

SUPERVISOR:       Directly responsible for your work. Assigns tasks,
                  monitors quality and safety, signs off completed work.

ARTISAN:          Qualified tradesperson. Responsible for own work quality
                  and the safety of those working alongside them.

APPRENTICE:       Learner on site. Must not work unsupervised on live
                  systems or high-risk tasks. Always work under direct
                  supervision until qualified.

CONTRACTOR:       External worker or company brought in for specific work.
                  Subject to site induction, permit requirements, and the
                  same safety rules as permanent staff.

                  zones. Must wear minimum PPE as required by site rules.
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why the Chain of Command Exists**
>
> The site hierarchy is not about rank for its own sake. It is an information management and accountability structure built to address a specific human failure mode: individuals who lack full system knowledge making decisions that affect systems they do not fully understand.
>
> An apprentice may be skilled and intelligent. But they do not yet have the full picture of how the systems they are working on interact with the rest of the facility. A supervisor does. The chain of command ensures that decisions with broad system consequences are made by people with broad system knowledge — or that the decision is escalated until it reaches someone who has it.
>
> The first principles test for any instruction you receive: *"Does the person giving this instruction have the authority and the system knowledge to give it safely?"* If you are genuinely unsure, the right response is to ask — not to assume.

#### Lesson 1.1.3 — How Work Gets Done: Planning, Authorisation, and Supervision (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The job card / work order system: how tasks are assigned and tracked
- The permit-to-work (PTW) system: why certain high-risk tasks require a signed permit before work begins
- Shift handover: why communication between outgoing and incoming shifts is safety-critical
- Lock-Out/Tag-Out (LOTO): introduced here as a concept (detailed in Unit 1.5)

**Work Order Anatomy:**

```
A WORK ORDER tells you:
  - What needs to be done (task description)
  - Where it needs to be done (location, equipment tag number)
  - By when (deadline)
  - Who authorised it (supervisor signature)
  - What permits are required before starting
  - What parts/materials have been allocated

You must NEVER start a job without a work order or verbal authorisation
from your supervisor. "Someone told me to do it" is not sufficient.
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why Work Orders Exist**
>
> Apply Q: *"Why can't I just start the job? I know what needs to be done."*
>
> The physical truth that work orders address: in a complex facility, the same piece of equipment may be connected to multiple systems, depended upon by multiple teams, and subject to multiple ongoing maintenance activities. A technician who starts work on a pump without a work order may not know that another team isolated that pump's control circuit an hour ago and is currently depending on it remaining isolated. The consequences of the interaction are physical — energy release, pressure change, system failure — and potentially fatal.
>
> A work order is a communication protocol that prevents uncoordinated physical interventions on shared systems. Its existence is derived from first principles: large systems have interdependencies that no single person can hold entirely in their head. Written authorisation and coordination protocols are the minimum viable solution to that physical reality.

## UNIT FND-IW-01.2 — Hazard Identification in Industrial Environments

**Duration:** 7 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Understand, Analyse
**Delivery:** Hazard classification instruction, image-based identification activity, 3D simulation

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Define the terms hazard, risk, and incident
- Classify a given hazard using a standard taxonomy of hazard types
- Identify the physical mechanism of harm for each hazard category
- Identify at least 15 discrete hazards in a 3D industrial environment
- Prioritise hazards by likelihood and severity using a basic risk matrix

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.2.1 — Hazard, Risk, Incident: What Is the Difference? (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Hazard: anything with the potential to cause harm
- Risk: the likelihood and severity of harm actually occurring given current conditions
- Incident: when something goes wrong (near-miss, injury, damage, spill)
- Why all three terms are used, why using them precisely matters legally and practically

**The Hazard-Risk-Incident Relationship:**

```
A HAZARD exists whether or not anyone is near it.
Example: A live electrical cable is always a hazard.

RISK changes depending on context.
Example: A live cable in a locked, sign-posted cabinet = low risk.
         A live cable lying on a wet floor in a busy walkway = extreme risk.

The same hazard can present very different levels of risk depending on:
  - Who is exposed (experience level, PPE, training)
  - How often they are exposed (frequency)
  - How severe the harm could be (severity)
  - Whether controls are in place (barriers, signage, isolation)
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Risk Is a Physical State, Not a Feeling**
>
> Many workers underestimate risk because the hazard looks familiar or because nothing has gone wrong before. This is the Q problem: the assumption is "familiar = safe." The first principles test is: *"What are the physical conditions present right now, and what is the physical mechanism by which they could cause harm?"*
>
> The live cable example above is instructive. The cable's hazard — its stored electrical energy — does not change whether it is in a locked cabinet or on a wet floor. What changes is the physical pathway by which that energy can reach a human body. On a wet floor, that pathway has low resistance and high probability of contact. In a locked cabinet, the pathway is physically blocked. Risk is a function of hazard plus pathway plus exposure — all of which are physical quantities, not feelings.
>
> This matters because it gives you a first principles method for assessing any situation you have never encountered before: identify the energy forms, identify the pathways, identify who is in those pathways, and you have a risk assessment that does not depend on having seen the situation before.

#### Lesson 1.2.2 — The Hazard Taxonomy (2 hours)

**Eight Hazard Categories — with First Principles Physical Mechanisms:**

```
1. PHYSICAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Objects, forces, or conditions that can cause direct
               physical injury
   Examples:   Moving machinery, falling objects, sharp edges, noise,
               vibration, extreme temperatures (hot surfaces, cold stores),
               radiation (UV from welding, ionising from X-ray machines),
               pressure vessels, rotating equipment

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Physical hazards cause harm through transfer of kinetic energy
   (impact, laceration), thermal energy (burns, frostbite), acoustic
   energy (hearing damage), or electromagnetic radiation (tissue damage).
   The physical question: what energy is present, at what magnitude,
   and what is the transfer pathway to the human body?

2. CHEMICAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Substances that can cause harm through contact,
               ingestion, or inhalation
   Examples:   Solvents, acids, alkalis, cleaning agents, lubricants,
               adhesives, battery acid, flammable liquids, pesticides,
               silica dust, asbestos fibres

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Chemical hazards cause harm through molecular interaction with
   biological tissue: acids donate protons that disrupt cell membranes;
   alkalis saponify fats in tissue; solvents dissolve lipid-based
   cell structures; fine particulates (silica, asbestos) physically
   lodge in lung alveoli and cause irreversible fibrosis. The entry
   route determines the organ at risk: inhalation targets lungs;
   skin contact targets dermal tissue; ingestion targets the GI tract.

3. ELECTRICAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Energised systems capable of causing shock, burns, fire,
               or explosion
   Examples:   Live conductors, damaged insulation, wet conditions near
               electrical equipment, overloaded circuits, capacitors
               (stored charge), arc flash, static discharge near flammables

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Electrical hazards cause harm through current flow through biological
   tissue. Current above approximately 10mA causes involuntary muscle
   contraction; above 100mA causes ventricular fibrillation (a
   potentially fatal disruption of heart rhythm); above 1A causes
   severe burns. The pathway matters: current between one hand and
   the other passes directly through the chest cavity and the heart.
   Arc flash additionally releases intense thermal radiation (plasma
   temperatures of 6000–20,000 K) and a pressure wave.

4. BIOLOGICAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Living organisms or organic materials that can cause
               illness or infection
   Examples:   Sewage and wastewater, food waste, mould, legionella in
               water systems, rodent droppings, contaminated sharps
               (healthcare facilities)

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Biological hazards cause harm through pathogen entry: viruses and
   bacteria that penetrate the body via inhalation, ingestion, skin
   puncture, or mucosal contact and replicate in host tissue. The
   physical control principle is containment of the entry route.

5. ERGONOMIC HAZARDS
   Definition: Conditions that cause physical strain over time
   Examples:   Repetitive motion, awkward postures, working at height,
               manual handling of heavy or awkward loads, whole-body
               vibration, poorly designed workstations

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Ergonomic hazards cause harm through cumulative mechanical stress
   on musculoskeletal structures. Tendons, cartilage, and intervertebral
   discs are viscoelastic materials with finite fatigue limits. Repeated
   loading at angles that exceed the tissue's designed load vector
   causes microscopic damage that accumulates faster than the body's
   repair rate. The harm is invisible until it becomes symptomatic —
   which is why ergonomic hazards are systematically underestimated.

6. PSYCHOSOCIAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Factors in the work environment that can affect
               mental health and behaviour
   Examples:   Excessive workload, poor supervision, workplace
               aggression, isolation (working alone in remote areas),
               shift work, fatigue

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Psychosocial hazards cause harm through sustained activation of the
   physiological stress response, which elevates cortisol, disrupts
   sleep architecture, impairs decision-making capacity, and increases
   error rates. Fatigue in particular is relevant to industrial safety:
   cognitively impaired workers make more assumption-based decisions
   (skipping the T step of QTB) and fewer verification-based ones.

7. ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS
   Definition: Conditions in the surrounding environment that create risk
   Examples:   Extreme weather (heat stress, lightning, flooding),
               unstable ground, poor visibility, confined spaces,
               working at height, working near water

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Environmental hazards modify the physical conditions under which
   all other hazards operate. Heat increases chemical reaction rates
   and impairs thermoregulation; lightning introduces an unpredictable
   high-current discharge pathway; confined spaces limit the dispersion
   of hazardous gases and prevent rapid evacuation; working near water
   provides a low-resistance electrical pathway to earth.

8. PROCESS/SYSTEM HAZARDS
   Definition: Hazards arising from how work is done, not just the
               physical environment
   Examples:   Concurrent work (two teams working on connected systems),
               energy stored in pressurised or charged systems,
               inadequate isolation before maintenance, system restart
               without clearance

   FIRST PRINCIPLES MECHANISM:
   Process hazards arise from the interaction between human cognitive
   limitations and complex system states. No individual can hold the
   full state of a large industrial system in working memory. Permits,
   LOTO, and coordination protocols are not bureaucratic overlays —
   they are compensatory mechanisms for a specific human cognitive
   constraint: limited working memory capacity relative to system
   complexity.
```

**Instructional Approach:**
- Each category is presented with a visual example gallery (12 real-world facility photos per category)
- Learners categorise 40 randomly shuffled hazard images using a drag-and-drop interface
- A "first principles" follow-on question accompanies each correctly categorised hazard: "Name the physical mechanism by which this hazard could cause harm." Learners select from three options; feedback explains the physical mechanism regardless of whether the answer is correct.

#### Lesson 1.2.3 — The Risk Matrix (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The 5x5 risk matrix as a prioritisation tool
- Likelihood scale (1 = Very Unlikely to 5 = Almost Certain)
- Severity scale (1 = Negligible to 5 = Catastrophic)
- Risk score = Likelihood × Severity
- Risk categories: Low (1–4), Medium (5–9), High (10–14), Extreme (15–25)

**Risk Matrix (5x5):**

```
                         SEVERITY
                  1       2       3       4       5
              Negligble Minor  Moderate Serious Catastrophic
            ┌───────┬───────┬───────┬───────┬───────┐
L  5 Almost │   5   │  10   │  15   │  20   │  25   │
I  Certain  ├───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┤
K  4 Likely │   4   │   8   │  12   │  16   │  20   │
E           ├───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┤
L  3 Possible│  3   │   6   │   9   │  12   │  15   │
I           ├───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┤
H  2 Unlikely│  2   │   4   │   6   │   8   │  10   │
O           ├───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┼───────┤
O 1 V.Unlikely│ 1  │   2   │   3   │   4   │   5   │
D           └───────┴───────┴───────┴───────┴───────┘

1–4: LOW (monitor, manage in routine procedures)
5–9: MEDIUM (action required; specific controls needed)
10–14: HIGH (work should not proceed without controls in place)
15–25: EXTREME (stop work; escalate immediately to supervisor)
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — What the Risk Matrix Is Actually Calculating**
>
> The risk score (Likelihood × Severity) is a simplified expected harm calculation. It estimates the average harm per exposure event across a population of workers over time. A high-severity, low-likelihood event (e.g., a catastrophic explosion) scores the same as a moderate-severity, moderate-likelihood event — and both warrant serious intervention — because the total harm potential is comparable.
>
> The first principles limitation to understand: the matrix is a tool for prioritisation, not for prediction. A risk score of 4 (Low) does not mean the event cannot happen — it means it is unlikely and the consequences are limited. An experienced worker applies QTB to their own risk assessment: *"Am I certain about the likelihood score? What assumption am I making about how well the existing controls are working?"* A control that appears to be functioning may not be — a machine guard that is always bypassed, for example, reduces the engineering control to zero, which changes the likelihood score significantly.

## UNIT FND-IW-01.3 — Risk Assessment Fundamentals

**Duration:** 6 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Understand, Apply
**Delivery:** Framework instruction, worked examples, written task

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Describe the hierarchy of controls and explain its ordering on first principles grounds
- Complete a basic job hazard analysis (JHA) for a simple task
- Distinguish between acceptable and unacceptable risk levels in a work context

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.3.1 — The Hierarchy of Controls (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Why the hierarchy exists: some controls are more reliable than others
- The five levels: Elimination, Substitution, Engineering Controls, Administrative Controls, PPE
- The critical insight: PPE is always the last resort, not the first response

**Hierarchy of Controls:**

```
MOST EFFECTIVE
     │
     ▼
1. ELIMINATION
   Remove the hazard entirely from the workplace.
   Example: Design the process so that no manual lifting is required.
   Reliability: Very high — the hazard cannot cause harm if it does not exist.

2. SUBSTITUTION
   Replace the hazardous material, process, or equipment with a
   less hazardous alternative.
   Example: Replace a solvent-based adhesive with a water-based one.
   Reliability: High — reduces hazard at the source.

3. ENGINEERING CONTROLS
   Physical changes to the work environment that reduce or contain
   the hazard.
   Example: Machine guards, ventilation systems, noise enclosures,
            cable routing through conduit instead of across the floor.
   Reliability: Good — does not rely on worker behaviour.

4. ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS
   Changes to how work is done: procedures, training, signage,
   scheduling, supervision.
   Example: Two-person rule for working at height; mandatory rest
            breaks in heat; restricted access zones.
   Reliability: Moderate — depends on worker compliance and
                consistent supervision.

5. PPE (PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT)
   Equipment worn by the worker to reduce exposure to residual risk.
   Example: Hard hats, safety boots, gloves, ear muffs, respirators.
   Reliability: Lowest — depends on correct selection, correct fit,
                consistent use, and maintenance of the equipment.
     │
     ▼
LEAST EFFECTIVE (but always necessary as backup)
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why the Hierarchy Is Ordered This Way**
>
> The hierarchy is not an arbitrary ranking. It is ordered by a single first principles criterion: *how many decisions by human beings does this control require in order to function?*
>
> Elimination requires zero decisions at the moment of hazard exposure — the hazard does not exist, so no human needs to remember to do anything. An engineering control (a machine guard) requires one decision to function: the decision to install it. Once installed, it works whether the worker thinks about it or not. An administrative control (a procedure) requires every worker, on every occasion, to remember and choose to follow it. PPE requires every worker, on every occasion, to select the correct item, don it correctly, maintain it, and wear it consistently.
>
> The more decisions required at the moment of exposure, the more opportunities there are for human cognitive failure — distraction, fatigue, overconfidence, time pressure. The hierarchy is a direct application of first principles to human reliability: reduce the dependence of safety on perfect human behaviour, because perfect human behaviour is not a physically achievable standard.
>
> **The practical implication:** When a supervisor offers PPE as the primary control for a serious hazard, the first principles question is: *"What is it about the physics of this situation that makes elimination, substitution, or an engineering control impossible here? If we cannot achieve those higher controls, how many additional administrative controls can we stack before PPE?"*

**Key Instructional Message:**
"If your only control for a serious hazard is PPE, your risk assessment is not complete. Ask: what engineering or administrative control can we add?"

- What a JHA is and when it is required (before any non-routine task; before any high-risk task regardless of routine)


> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — The JHA as a QTB Walk-Through**
>
> Each row of a JHA is a T step applied to one moment in a task: *"What is physically true about this step? What energy forms are present? What pathways to harm exist?"* The Control Measures column is a B step: *"Given this physical truth, what is the minimum intervention that reduces the harm pathway to an acceptable level?"*

## UNIT FND-IW-01.4 — Personal Protective Equipment

**Duration:** 5 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Understand, Apply
**Delivery:** Reference guide, visual identification activity, 3D simulation

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Identify 12 categories of PPE and describe the hazard each protects against
- Explain the physical principle by which each PPE item provides protection
- Select the correct PPE for a specified task and environment
- Demonstrate correct donning, doffing, and inspection procedures for basic PPE

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.4.1 — PPE Categories, Functions, and Physical Principles (1 hour 30 min)

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — PPE as Applied Material Science**
>
> Before listing PPE categories, establish the first principles foundation: every item of PPE works by creating a physical barrier or energy absorber between a hazardous energy source and the body part at risk. The choice of PPE material is derived directly from the physical properties of the hazard. Choosing the wrong material — a dust mask against chemical vapours, cotton gloves against electrical shock — provides no protection whatsoever, because the physical mechanism of protection does not match the physical mechanism of harm.
>
> For every PPE item, learners should be able to answer: *"What is the physical property of this material that stops this specific form of energy from reaching my tissue?"* This is the T step applied to PPE selection: confirm the physical compatibility between the material and the hazard before relying on it.

**The 12 PPE Categories — with Physical Principles:**

```
1.  HEAD PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Hard hat (Class A/B/C/E — explain classification)
    Protects against: Falling objects, bump hazards, electrical
    Physical principle: Hard outer shell distributes impact force
                        over a larger area, reducing peak pressure
                        on skull. Suspension system inside absorbs
                        kinetic energy through controlled deformation.
    Key rule:   Replace after any impact — the shell may have
                micro-fractured, compromising its energy distribution.
                Fractures are often invisible externally.

2.  EYE AND FACE PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Safety glasses, goggles, face shield
    Protects against: Projectiles, splashing chemicals, UV radiation,
                      arc flash
    Physical principle: Safety glass is tempered or polycarbonate —
                        materials with higher fracture toughness than
                        standard glass. Goggles create a sealed chamber;
                        liquids cannot enter via gaps that standard glasses
                        leave open. Face shields redirect arc flash radiation
                        away from facial tissue.
    Key rule:   Glasses ≠ goggles; chemical work requires sealed goggles
                because the splash entry vector is lateral, not frontal.

3.  HEARING PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Disposable foam earplugs (NRR rated), earmuffs
    Protects against: Noise-induced hearing loss (irreversible)
    Physical principle: Foam earplugs attenuate sound energy by
                        damping the pressure wave before it reaches
                        the tympanic membrane. Earmuffs add an air gap
                        that further reduces transmission. Attenuation
                        is measured in decibels (dB) of Noise Reduction
                        Rating (NRR). Hearing loss is permanent — cochlear
                        hair cells do not regenerate in humans.
    Key rule:   Required when noise exceeds 85 dB. Foam plugs must
                be fully expanded and seated in the ear canal to achieve
                rated attenuation — partial insertion provides far less
                protection than the NRR label suggests.

4.  RESPIRATORY PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Dust mask (N95/P2), half-face respirator, full-face
                with cartridge, SCBA (Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus)
    Protects against: Dust, fumes, gases, vapours
    Physical principle: P2/N95 masks use electrostatic filtration —
                        fibres carry a static charge that attracts
                        particles smaller than the physical gap between
                        fibres. This is why they work on particles too
                        small to be mechanically filtered. However,
                        electrostatic filtration does not work on gases
                        or vapours (molecular-scale substances that pass
                        straight through). Chemical cartridge respirators
                        use activated carbon that adsorbs specific gases —
                        different cartridges for different chemicals.
    Key rule:   Match the mask to the hazard at the molecular level.
                A P2 mask on a solvent vapour provides zero protection.

5.  HAND PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Leather gloves (mechanical), nitrile gloves (chemical),
                insulated gloves (electrical), cut-resistant gloves
    Protects against: Cuts, abrasions, chemical burns, electrical shock
    Physical principle: Leather provides high tensile and abrasion
                        resistance (collagen fibre matrix). Nitrile
                        resists non-polar solvents that would dissolve
                        latex (similar polarity = dissolution). Insulated
                        gloves have a dielectric rubber layer that prevents
                        current flow at rated voltage.
    Key rule:   Never wear gloves near rotating machinery — entanglement
                risk (the glove grips the rotating part and draws the
                hand in). The physical harm from entanglement exceeds
                the harm the glove was preventing.

6.  FOOT PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Safety boots (steel toe cap, anti-slip sole, ankle support)
    Protects against: Crushing, punctures, slipping, chemical spills
    Physical principle: Steel toe cap distributes and limits the
                        compressive force reaching the toes from a
                        dropped object. Anti-slip sole uses a rubber
                        compound with a specific surface texture to
                        maximise friction coefficient on wet or oily
                        floors. Anti-static versions have a resistive
                        (not insulating) sole that bleeds static charge
                        to earth slowly, preventing buildup.
    Key rule:   Leather uppers only in chemical environments;
                anti-static boots required near electronics/explosive areas.

7.  BODY/SKIN PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Overalls, chemical splash suit, flame-retardant coveralls,
                high-visibility vest
    Protects against: Chemical splash, heat, fire, low-visibility conditions
    Physical principle: Flame-retardant fabrics are treated with compounds
                        that interrupt the combustion chain reaction —
                        they do not prevent ignition but self-extinguish
                        when the ignition source is removed. Hi-vis materials
                        use fluorescent pigments (active in daylight) and
                        retroreflective strips (active in artificial light)
                        to reflect light back toward its source — maximising
                        contrast against the background environment.
    Key rule:   Hi-vis must be worn in all vehicle movement areas.

8.  FALL PROTECTION
    Equipment:  Full body harness, lanyard, self-retracting lifeline,
                anchor points
    Protects against: Falling from height (>2m trigger under SA OHS)
    Physical principle: A harness converts a free fall (uncontrolled
                        kinetic energy release) into an arrested fall
                        (controlled deceleration via the lanyard's energy
                        absorber). The energy absorber is typically a
                        packed webbing that tears in a controlled way —
                        extending the deceleration distance and reducing
                        peak force on the body below the threshold for
                        skeletal injury (typically ~6 kN).
    Key rule:   Harness must be inspected before every use. A
                lanyard that has been loaded in a fall must be replaced —
                the energy absorber has been consumed and will not function
                again.

9.  HEIGHT SAFETY FOOTWEAR
    Equipment:  Steel-capped, slip-resistant work boots with ankle support
    Note:       Covered under foot protection but specifically required
                for working on elevated platforms

10. ELECTRICAL INSULATION PPE
    Equipment:  Insulated gloves (Class 0–4 rated to voltage),
                insulated mats, face shields for arc flash,
                flame-resistant clothing
    Protects against: Electric shock, arc flash, blast
    Physical principle: Dielectric rubber has extremely high resistivity
                        (~10¹³–10¹⁵ Ω·m), creating an effective open
                        circuit between the energised conductor and the
                        wearer's body. Voltage class rating specifies
                        the maximum voltage at which the insulating
                        layer remains intact. Arc-rated clothing is
                        rated in cal/cm² of incident energy it can
                        withstand before igniting.
    Key rule:   Insulated gloves must be dielectrically tested every
                six months. Test for pin holes by inflating the glove
                and checking for air leakage — a 1mm pinhole provides
                a current pathway that renders the glove useless.

11. HIGH VISIBILITY (HI-VIS)
    Equipment:  Class 1, 2, or 3 vest or jacket
    Protects against: Being struck by moving vehicles or plant
    Physical principle: See Body/Skin Protection above. Class
                        rating determines minimum retroreflective
                        strip area — higher class = more reflective
                        material = visible at greater distances and
                        lower light levels.
    Key rule:   Mandatory in all plant, loading, and roadwork areas.

12. SUNSCREEN AND UV PROTECTION
    Equipment:  SPF 30+ sunscreen, UV-blocking safety glasses,
                wide-brim hard hat, long sleeves
    Protects against: UV-induced skin damage (occupational skin cancer)
    Physical principle: SPF-rated sunscreen contains UV-absorbing
                        compounds (organic filters) or UV-reflecting
                        compounds (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) that
                        reduce the ultraviolet radiation dose reaching
                        the skin. UV damage to DNA is cumulative and
                        dose-dependent — there is no recovery between
                        exposures above the repair threshold.
    Key rule:   Required for all outdoor work exceeding 30 minutes
                between 10:00 and 14:00
```

**Duration:** 5 hours

**Delivery:** Document study, scenario-based test

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Explain the purpose and structure of a permit-to-work (PTW) system
- Describe the Lock-Out/Tag-Out (LOTO) procedure and explain why it must not be bypassed — stated in physical and human terms
- Identify the correct response procedure for a site emergency (fire, spill, medical)
- Describe the legal obligations of a worker under the South African OHS Act

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.5.1 — The Permit-to-Work System (1 hour 30 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- Why PTW exists: preventing concurrent hazardous work on shared systems
- Types of permits: Hot Work Permit, Confined Space Entry Permit, Electrical Isolation Permit, Working at Height Permit, Excavation Permit
- The permit process: application → hazard assessment → approval → briefing → issue → execution → clearance → close-out
- What a permit does NOT do: it does not make work safe by itself; it is a communication and authorisation tool

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why the Permit-to-Work System Exists**
>
> Apply Q: *"Why can't I just tell my supervisor what I'm doing and start? That seems like enough communication."*
>
> The physical truth that the PTW addresses is a specific failure mode: **concurrent work on interdependent systems by people who do not know each other's activities.** In a large facility, it is entirely possible for Team A to be working on a valve in a pipe system while Team B is simultaneously working on the pressure source for that same system, assuming the other end is isolated. Neither team is negligent. Both are doing their job. The failure is systemic — it cannot be prevented by making individuals more careful. It can only be prevented by a system-level communication protocol.
>
> The permit is not a bureaucratic form. It is the mechanism by which the full system state — what is isolated, what is live, who is working where, what cannot be touched — is made visible to everyone whose safety depends on it. This is B applied to system coordination: the physical truth of a large, complex system cannot be held in any one person's head. The PTW externalises it.

**Permit-to-Work Process Flow:**

```
WORKER identifies high-risk task
        │
        ▼
WORKER completes permit application form (task, location, method, hazards, controls)
        │
        ▼
SUPERVISOR reviews and signs (if approved)
        │
        ▼
SAFETY OFFICER reviews (for specified high-risk categories)
        │
        ▼
AFFECTED PARTIES notified (other crews, operators of adjacent systems)
        │
        ▼
WORK BEGINS (permit displayed at worksite)
        │
        ▼
WORK COMPLETED → site cleaned and restored → supervisor signs off
        │
        ▼
PERMIT CLOSED (filed; systems restored to normal operation)
```

**Non-Negotiable Rules:**
- A permit is not transferable: the person named on it must be present
- A permit that is suspended (e.g., for a shift break or emergency) must be re-verified before work resumes
- Starting hot work without a permit in an industrial facility is a dismissible offence and a criminal liability

#### Lesson 1.5.2 — Lock-Out/Tag-Out (LOTO) (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The single most common cause of severe industrial injuries: working on equipment that restarts unexpectedly
- What LOTO does: physically locks energy sources in the off or isolated state
- Energy sources covered by LOTO: electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, gravitational, thermal, stored mechanical
- The personal padlock principle

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why LOTO Cannot Be Shortcut**
>
> Apply Q: *"Why can't I just tell the operator not to start the machine? That should be enough."*
>
> The physical and human truth that LOTO addresses: **verbal agreements do not hold against the full range of human failure modes.** The operator you told might be replaced by a different operator at shift change. The operator might forget in the 40 minutes since you spoke. A different team might issue a restart command through a different control panel without knowing you are inside the machine. An automated control system might issue a start signal on a timer.
>
> LOTO works because it creates a physical impossibility, not a social agreement. A padlocked isolator switch cannot be closed by anyone, for any reason, as long as the lock is in place — regardless of urgency, pressure, communication failure, or human error. The physical barrier substitutes for perfect human coordination. This is the same first principles logic as engineering controls in the hierarchy: the physical state of the system is more reliable than human behaviour.
>
> **The personal padlock principle derived from first principles:** *If multiple people are working on the same equipment, one collective lock does not protect each individual — it protects only the person holding the key. A second worker assumes the collective lock means the system is safe, then the key-holder removes the lock believing all workers have cleared, and the second worker is still inside. A personal lock eliminates this failure mode physically: the equipment cannot be re-energised until every individual worker has personally confirmed their own clearance by removing their own lock.*

**The LOTO Procedure (7 Steps):**

```
STEP 1: IDENTIFY all energy sources for the equipment to be worked on
        (Do not assume — check the electrical single-line diagram and
         the machine's energy isolation register)

STEP 2: NOTIFY all affected personnel that the equipment is being
        isolated and why

STEP 3: SHUT DOWN the equipment using its normal operating procedure

STEP 4: ISOLATE all energy sources (switch off isolator, close valve,
        release stored pressure)

STEP 5: LOCK OUT each isolation point with a personal padlock;
        attach a personal tag with your name, date, and contact number

STEP 6: VERIFY isolation by attempting to start the equipment (press
        start button); test for live voltage with an approved meter;
        bleed pressure from hydraulic/pneumatic lines

STEP 7: ONLY THEN begin work
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES NOTE ON STEP 6:**
>
> Step 6 is the T step of QTB applied to LOTO: it requires you to confirm the physical state of the system by direct measurement, not by assumption. The reason you test for voltage after locking out is that assumptions about isolation have killed people. A breaker may appear to be open but may have failed in the closed position. A valve may appear closed but may be leaking under pressure. Physical verification — the meter, the pressure gauge, the start button test — confirms what the T step requires: a fact, not an assumption.

**After Work is Complete:**

```
STEP 8:  Remove all tools and materials from the work area
STEP 9:  Replace all guards and panels
STEP 10: Remove your personal lock and tag only
STEP 11: Notify supervisor that work is complete and system is ready
          for re-energisation
STEP 12: Supervisor verifies and authorises re-energisation
```

#### Lesson 1.5.3 — Emergency Response Protocols (1 hour)

**Emergency Types Covered:** Fire, chemical spill, medical emergency, electrical incident, evacuation

```
FIRE RESPONSE — RACE:
  R — RESCUE:    Get anyone in immediate danger away from the fire if safe to do so
  A — ALERT:     Sound the alarm; call emergency number; notify supervisor
  C — CONTAIN:   Close doors to slow the spread (do not lock)
  E — EVACUATE:  Exit via designated route; do not use lifts; go to assembly point

FIRE EXTINGUISHER USE — PASS:
  P — PULL the pin
  A — AIM at the base of the fire
  S — SQUEEZE the handle
  S — SWEEP from side to side
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why You Aim at the Base of a Fire**
>
> A fire is a sustained chemical chain reaction: fuel reacts with oxygen in the presence of heat to produce combustion products and more heat. The chain reaction sustains itself because the heat output re-ignites fresh fuel. Aiming at the flames (the visible heat) addresses the symptom. Aiming at the base — where unburned fuel meets the ignition zone — disrupts the chain reaction at its source. A dry chemical or CO₂ extinguisher at the base eliminates or displaces the oxygen and smothers the reaction. This is B applied to fire suppression: derived from the physical chemistry of combustion, not from a procedural rule.

#### Lesson 1.5.4 — Your Legal Rights and Responsibilities (1 hour)

**Concepts Covered:**
- South African OHS Act (No. 85 of 1993): what it requires of workers
- Your right to refuse unsafe work (Section 28)
- Your duty to report hazards (Section 14)
- The role of the Health and Safety Representative on site

**Key Worker Obligations Under OHS Act Section 14:**

```
As a worker, you MUST:

a) Take reasonable care for the health and safety of yourself and others
   who may be affected by your actions or omissions

b) Cooperate with your employer on matters of health and safety

c) Carry out lawful instructions given by your employer or supervisor
   regarding health and safety

d) Report any unsafe or unhealthy conditions to your employer or
   health and safety representative as soon as possible

e) Report any accident or incident to your supervisor as soon as
   possible after it occurs — even if no one was hurt (near misses
   must be reported)

f) NOT interfere with, damage, or misuse any health and safety
   equipment provided by your employer
```

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why Near-Miss Reporting Is Non-Negotiable**
>
> A near-miss is a T step the industrial system failed to perform — a hazardous condition that existed and was not detected until it was almost too late. Reporting a near-miss is not a bureaucratic obligation. It is the provision of physical evidence that the current controls are insufficient. A near-miss that goes unreported represents a hazard condition that persists in the system. The first incident was a warning. The second may be an injury.
>
> The first principles case for near-miss reporting: *"A near-miss is the cheapest possible source of information about a system failure. It costs no injury, no death, and no insurance claim. Failing to report it means paying a much higher price for the same information at the next occurrence."*

#### Lesson 1.6.1 — Hand Tools Reference Library (2 hours)

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Tool Design Is Applied Physics**
>
> Every hand tool is an engineered solution to a specific physical problem. Its shape, material, and geometry are derived from the physics of the task it performs. Understanding the first principles basis for a tool's design tells you three things simultaneously: what it is for, how to use it correctly, and why misusing it will either damage the tool, the workpiece, or yourself.
>
> Apply QTB to every unfamiliar tool: *"What physical problem is this designed to solve? What physical principle does its geometry exploit? What happens to the physics if I use it in a way it was not designed for?"*

**Tool Categories and Specific Tools — with First Principles Rationale:**

```
CATEGORY 1: FASTENING TOOLS

  1.  Flat-head screwdriver
      Physical principle: Converts rotational torque into clamping force
                          via the screw thread (an inclined plane wrapped
                          into a helix). The flat blade fits the slot to
                          transfer torque without slipping.
      Use: Driving or removing flat-slot screws
      Misuse: Using as a pry bar — the blade is hardened for torsion,
              not bending; lateral force causes the tip to snap. Using
              as a chisel — the handle is not designed for axial impact.

  2.  Phillips screwdriver (PH0, PH1, PH2, PH3)
      Physical principle: The cross-shaped profile was designed to
                          self-centre and allow the driver to cam out
                          under excessive torque — preventing overtightening
                          on assembly lines. This cam-out feature is a
                          design choice, not a defect. It becomes a problem
                          only when a size mismatch reduces the contact area.
      Use: Phillips-head screws
      Misuse: Using wrong size — reduces contact patch, concentrates force
              at screw edges, strips the head on the first application of
              real torque

  3.  Pozidriv screwdriver (PZ1, PZ2)
      Physical principle: Pozidriv adds four additional contact surfaces
                          at 45° to the primary cross — doubling the torque
                          transfer surface area and eliminating the cam-out
                          behaviour of Phillips. The two profiles are not
                          interchangeable at the physics level.
      Use: Pozidriv-head screws (common in South African electrical fittings)
      Misuse: Using a Phillips in a Pozidriv — the PH blade contacts only
              4 surfaces; the PZ has 8. The mismatched blade rocks under
              torque and strips both the screw and the driver tip.

  4.  Allen key / Hex key set (metric: 1.5mm–10mm)
      Physical principle: The hexagonal socket distributes torque evenly
                          across 6 flat contact faces — maximising the
                          torque that can be applied before slipping.
                          Six flat faces in full contact = minimal stress
                          concentration per face.
      Use: Socket-head cap screws
      Misuse: Using wrong size (rounds off hex socket — removes contact
              surface area progressively with each application of torque)

  5.  Combination spanner set (8mm–24mm)
      Physical principle: The ring end grips all six faces of a hex nut
                          simultaneously; the open end grips only two
                          opposing faces. Under high torque, the ring end
                          applies force to all six faces evenly (no
                          slipping); the open end concentrates force on
                          two faces (can round the nut if the spanner fits
                          loosely or force is excessive).
      Use: Hexagonal nuts and bolts
      Misuse: Using open end alone for high torque — the two-face contact
              generates high local stress and will round a soft nut

  6.  Adjustable spanner (Crescent wrench)
      Physical principle: The adjustable jaw must be tight against the nut
                          face before applying force. If loose, the jaw
                          flexes under load, reducing contact area and
                          rounding the nut. The direction of pull matters:
                          pulling toward the fixed jaw loads the jaw hinge
                          in tension (stable); pulling toward the adjustable
                          jaw loads it in the opening direction (unstable).
      Use: Nuts and fittings where a fixed spanner is not available
      Misuse: Pulling in the wrong direction; using when a correct fixed
              spanner is available (an adjustable spanner is never as
              precise as a matched fixed spanner)

CATEGORY 2: CUTTING TOOLS

  7.  Junior hacksaw
      Physical principle: Each tooth removes a small chip of material
                          in one direction (the forward stroke). Tooth
                          set (the alternating left-right offset of teeth)
                          creates a kerf slightly wider than the blade
                          body, preventing the blade from jamming.
      Use: Cutting metal conduit, bolts, rods up to 25mm
      Misuse: Cutting too fast — generates heat that softens the blade's
              temper, dulling it permanently; also work-hardens the cut
              surface, making it even harder to cut

  8.  Stanley knife / utility knife
      Physical principle: A sharp edge concentrates cutting force on an
                          extremely small area, generating very high local
                          stress that exceeds the material's tensile
                          strength. A dull edge distributes that force
                          over a larger area — requiring more total force
                          to achieve the same cutting stress. More force =
                          less control = higher injury risk.
      Safety rule: Cut away from the body; retract blade when not in use;
                   a sharp blade is safer than a dull one (counterintuitive
                   but physically accurate)

  9.  Wire strippers (adjustable jaw)
      Physical principle: The cutting jaw is set to cut through the
                          insulation polymer without reaching the conductor.
                          The conductor material (copper, aluminium) is
                          softer than the blade but any nick creates a
                          stress concentration point in the metal. Under
                          current load, a nicked conductor runs hotter at
                          the nick (higher resistance at reduced cross-section)
                          and may eventually fracture or overheat.
      Misuse: Using a knife — a knife cannot be reliably controlled to the
              precision required; nicking is almost inevitable

CATEGORY 3: GRIPPING AND FORMING TOOLS

  10. Combination pliers (200mm)
      Physical principle: The jaw pivot creates a mechanical advantage
                          that multiplies the grip force applied at the
                          handles. Serrated jaw faces increase friction
                          force at the grip point, preventing slip under
                          high tangential forces.
      Misuse: Using as a hammer — pliers are hardened for torsion and
              grip loads, not axial impact; the pivot can shear

  11. Long-nose pliers
      Physical principle: The elongated jaw geometry allows reaching into
                          spaces narrower than the hand, while the tapered
                          tip concentrates grip force on small components.
                          The jaw is not designed to carry the same torque
                          loads as combination pliers — the long lever arm
                          increases bending moment on the jaws.
      Misuse: Using as a spanner — concentrates bending moment at jaw tip;
              snaps the jaw

  12. Side-cutting pliers (Dikes)
      Physical principle: The blades are angled to allow flush cutting close
                          to a surface. The cutting edge is hardened but
                          brittle at the micro-scale — designed for copper
                          and aluminium, not steel wire or bolts.
      Misuse: Cutting hard materials — the hardened cutting edge chips or
              deforms permanently on materials harder than its design rating

CATEGORY 4: MEASURING TOOLS

  13. Steel rule (300mm / 600mm)
      Physical principle: Measurement accuracy depends on aligning the
                          rule edge — not the face — with the datum surface.
                          When the rule lies flat, parallax error from
                          reading at an angle can introduce errors of 0.5mm
                          or more. Standing the rule on its edge eliminates
                          parallax.
      Use: Measuring and marking; standing on edge for line marking

  14. Tape measure (3m / 5m)
      Physical principle: The hook at the end of the tape slides by
                          approximately the thickness of the hook material
                          — this compensates for the hook's own thickness
                          when measuring from an inside corner (hook
                          against the surface) vs. an outside edge (hook
                          over the end). This compensation is precise and
                          intentional, not a manufacturing defect.

  15. Spirit level (400mm / 600mm)
      Physical principle: The vial contains a liquid (usually ethanol or
                          oil) with a small air bubble. Gravity acts on the
                          denser liquid, forcing it to the low point of the
                          vial — the bubble, being less dense, rises to the
                          highest point. When the surface is perfectly level,
                          the highest point is geometrically the centre of
                          the curved vial. The bubble position is a direct
                          physical indicator of the gravitational
                          perpendicular, not a calibration convention.
      Bubble interpretation: Centre = level; drifts toward low side

  16. Vernier caliper
      Physical principle: The vernier scale exploits the fact that the
                          human eye can detect the alignment of two lines
                          more accurately than it can estimate the fractional
                          position of one line between two others. The 0.02mm
                          resolution of a vernier caliper is achieved not by
                          mechanical precision alone but by this optical
                          interpolation method.
      Use: Precise measurement of wire diameter, conduit inner diameter,
           bolt dimensions (0.02mm precision)

  17. Digital multimeter (Fluke or equivalent)
      Physical principle: Voltmeters measure potential difference between
                          two points; they have very high internal resistance
                          (~10 MΩ) so they draw negligible current from the
                          circuit under test. Ammeters have very low
                          resistance and must be placed in series. Connecting
                          a voltmeter in series or an ammeter in parallel
                          produces incorrect readings and, in the case of an
                          ammeter in parallel across a voltage source, a
                          dead short through the meter — destroying it
                          instantly.
      Safety rule: Check CAT category rating against the system being tested

CATEGORY 5: MARKING TOOLS

  18. Combination square
      Physical principle: The blade slides and locks in a cast iron or
                          hardened stock. The stock has two reference faces:
                          one at 90° and one at 45° to the blade. The 90°
                          face transfers the perpendicular reference of the
                          blade edge to the workpiece surface. Accuracy
                          depends on the stock face being clean and the
                          blade being fully tightened before marking.

  19. Centre punch
      Physical principle: The hardened, ground tip concentrates the impact
                          force of a hammer blow onto a very small area,
                          generating local stress that plastically deforms
                          the metal surface into a small conical indentation.
                          This indentation self-centres the drill bit tip,
                          preventing it from wandering across the surface
                          as the drill begins to bite.

  20. Chalk line / snap line
      Physical principle: The chalk-coated string, when pulled taut, lies
                          along a perfect straight line defined by its two
                          anchor points (a geometric straight line is the
                          shortest distance between two points — the taut
                          string instantiates this physically). Lifting
                          and releasing the string at its midpoint causes
                          it to snap against the surface, transferring the
                          chalk at the contact point along the full length.
```

**Scenario Brief:**
You have been given three small tasks in the workshop. For each task, you must select the correct tool(s) from the shadow board, demonstrate correct use technique, and return each tool to its correct position when done.

**Task 1 — Measure and Mark:**
Mark a cable tray mounting position on the wall: 1.5m from the floor, level, with four fixing holes at correct spacing. Required tools: tape measure, spirit level, pencil, combination square, centre punch.

**Task 2 — Cut and Terminate a Cable Length:**
A 1.5m length of 2.5mm² twin-and-earth cable must be cut to length and both ends stripped to 12mm. Required tools: tape measure, Stanley knife (for outer sheath), wire strippers (for conductor insulation).

**Task 3 — Assemble a Bracket:**
Mount a wall bracket using M6 bolts and nuts. Tighten to firm but not over-torqued. Required tools: combination spanner (correct size identified by learner), Allen key (correct size).

**First Principles Integration in Simulation:**
Before selecting each tool, the learner must complete a short QTB entry:
- Q: What assumption am I making about which tool to use?
- T: What is the physical task I need to accomplish (cut, grip, measure, fasten)?
- B: Which tool's physical design principle matches that task?

Wrong tool selections trigger a prompt that asks: *"This tool is not the right choice. Think about the physical task: what must happen to the workpiece? What physical principle does the correct tool need to apply?"*

**Simulation Features:**
- Tool selection from shadow board: incorrect tool selection triggers a physics-based explanation prompt
- Technique validation: checks for correct sequence (e.g., centre punch before drill; wire strippers not Stanley knife for conductor insulation)
- Torque feedback: over-tightening triggers a "stripped thread" visual and audio cue with explanation of the failure mechanism; under-tightening triggers a "loose fastener" warning
- All tools rendered with PBR materials: steel with micro-abrasion detail, rubber grip with surface texture, chrome-vanadium finish on spanners

**Scoring:**
- QTB pre-selection entries (physical reasoning present): 20%
- Correct tool selected for each sub-task: 30%
- Technique sequence followed correctly: 25%
- Measurements within acceptable tolerance (±2mm): 15%
- All tools returned to correct shadow board positions: 10%

## UNIT FND-IW-01.7 — Pre-Use Inspection and Tool Care

**Duration:** 4 hours
**Bloom's Focus:** Apply
**Delivery:** Inspection procedure instruction, practical checklist task

### Unit Learning Outcomes

- Conduct a pre-use inspection of a hand tool using a standard inspection checklist
- Identify and correctly classify defects as "use with caution," "tag out for repair," or "condemn and replace"
- Explain the physical failure mechanism of each defect type
- Describe correct cleaning, storage, and maintenance procedures for five tool categories

### Content Outline

#### Lesson 1.7.1 — Why Tool Inspection Matters (45 min)

**Concepts Covered:**
- The three failure modes: gradual wear (predictable), sudden failure (unpredictable from use), and misuse damage (preventable)
- The cost of using a defective tool: injury to self, injury to others, damage to workpiece, legal liability
- The "tag out" system

> **FIRST PRINCIPLES LENS — Why Defects Matter at a Physical Level**
>
> Every defect in a tool represents a change in the physical properties that made the tool safe and functional. A cracked handle does not just "look bad" — it has a stress concentration at the crack tip that reduces the load the handle can carry before fracturing by a factor that depends on the crack geometry and the material's fracture toughness. Under the same load that the intact handle would carry safely, the cracked handle may fail suddenly and catastrophically.
>
> A cut in insulation does not just "look worn" — it provides a pathway for current flow that the insulation was designed to prevent. The electrical resistance of the exposed region drops from ~10¹⁵ Ω·m to the resistance of the surrounding air (much lower) or of the worker's damp skin (much lower still).
>
> The T step of QTB applied to tool inspection: *"What are the physical properties of this tool that make it safe? Do those properties still exist in this specific specimen, based on what I can observe?"*

#### Lesson 1.7.2 — Pre-Use Inspection Procedure (1 hour)

**The Five-Step Pre-Use Inspection:**

```
STEP 1: VISUAL CHECK (overall condition)
        - Look for cracks, deformation, corrosion, missing parts
        - Check for oil, grease, or chemical contamination on handles
        - Check that all components are present

STEP 2: HANDLE AND GRIP CHECK
        - Grip must be firm and non-slip
        - No cracks, splinters, or looseness
        - Insulated handles must be free of cuts, abrasions, and swelling
          (insulation failure is often invisible until it is fatal)

STEP 3: WORKING SURFACE CHECK
        - Cutting edges: check for chips, deformation, excessive wear
        - Gripping surfaces: check jaw alignment and tooth condition
        - Measuring surfaces: check for burrs, corrosion, and zero error

STEP 4: FASTENER CHECK
        - Screws holding handles must be tight
        - Adjustable components must lock securely
        - No play or wobble in any joint

STEP 5: FUNCTION CHECK
        - Open and close pliers through full range of movement
        - Extend and retract tape measure through full length
        - Check knife blade deploys, retracts, and locks
```

**Defect Classification — with Physical Failure Mechanisms:**

```
GREEN — USE WITH CAUTION:
    Minor cosmetic surface rust on non-contact area
    Physical mechanism: Surface oxidation of iron to iron oxide. At
                        early stages, limited to the surface layer. Does
                        not significantly reduce cross-section or load
                        capacity. Monitor — deep pitting rust does.
    Slight discolouration on handle (no structural damage)
    Action: Log the observation; monitor at next inspection

YELLOW — TAG OUT FOR REPAIR:
    Loose handle screw
    Physical mechanism: Screw has lost clamping force. Under torque or
                        impact, the handle may rotate relative to the
                        blade, reducing force transfer and causing slip.
    Minor chip on cutting edge
    Physical mechanism: A chip in a hardened cutting edge creates a
                        stress riser at the chip boundary. Further use
                        may propagate the chip into a larger fracture.
                        Re-grind removes the stress riser.
    Stiff pivot joint
    Physical mechanism: Corrosion or debris in the pivot creates friction
                        that requires more hand force to achieve the same
                        grip force — increasing fatigue and reducing
                        control. Clean and lubricate restores designed
                        mechanical advantage.
    Action: Remove from use; attach yellow tag; report to supervisor

RED — CONDEMN AND REPLACE:
    Cracked or splintered handle
    Physical mechanism: A crack tip in a polymer or wood handle reduces
                        the effective load-bearing cross-section and
                        creates a stress concentration factor that
                        increases local stress by 2–5× for the same applied
                        load. Sudden catastrophic fracture under normal
                        use force is physically plausible.
    Cut, split, or swollen insulation on electrician's tools
    Physical mechanism: Any breach in the insulating layer creates a
                        current pathway to the user. Swelling indicates
                        chemical or moisture ingress that has already
                        compromised the dielectric properties internally.
    Blade that does not lock securely
    Physical mechanism: An unlocked blade under cutting force will fold
                        back at the pivot under load, cutting the user's
                        hand.
    Tape measure with kinked or buckled tape
    Physical mechanism: A kink in the spring steel tape creates a plastic
                        deformation point. The tape will not extend straight
                        past this point, introducing a measurement error at
                        that location.
    Any measuring tool that cannot be zeroed
    Physical mechanism: All measurements derived from this tool contain
                        a systematic error of unknown magnitude. Every
                        dimension marked with it will be wrong by that
                        error — multiplied across every hole, every cut,
                        every bracket position.
    Action: Remove from use; attach red DO NOT USE tag;
            inform supervisor; do not leave on bench
```

#### Assessment: Practical Checklist Task (2 hours 15 min)

**Task Brief:**
Learners are presented with a virtual tool inspection station. Eight tools are displayed — each has been subtly defected in the simulation. Learners conduct a full five-step pre-use inspection on each tool using the digital inspection checklist and classify each defect.

**Deliverables:**
1. Completed inspection checklist for all eight tools (40 fields)
2. Classification for each defect found (Green/Yellow/Red)
3. Physical failure mechanism explanation for each defect (one sentence: "This defect matters because...")
4. A short written entry (max 50 words per tool) into a simulated maintenance log describing the defect, its physical mechanism, and recommended action

**Rubric:**
- Defect identification accuracy: 35%
- Classification correctness: 25%
- Physical failure mechanism explanation is accurate: 20%
- Maintenance log entries are specific, clear, physically grounded, and actionable: 20%
